# Series-Level Comprehension Guide

This document describes the comprehension work that happens during series design — reading a body of research as a whole to discover what it makes visible across multiple articles.

---

## The Distinction

**Series-level comprehension (this skill):** What does this body of research add up to? Where are the natural article boundaries? What structural role does each article play in the series build? What does each article carry that later articles depend on?

**Article-level comprehension (drafting-articles skill):** What does the evidence for *this specific article* actually say? How do the pieces connect? What argument does the evidence earn? What's the structural plan for building that argument?

The series design skill does the first. The drafting skill does the second (Comprehend understands the evidence; Design finds the story and builds the structural plan). The boundary matters because over-comprehending at the series level — building per-article argument sequences, pre-determining section structures, pre-selecting evidence for specific sections — produces a series map that anchors the drafting agent on compressed conclusions rather than on fresh reasoning from the research.

---

## What Series-Level Comprehension Produces

For each article in the series:

- **What it carries** — the structural payload this article delivers. Not what it argues — what the reader gains that they need for later articles. This is a series-build concern, not an argument-structure concern.

- **Argument arc** — how the evidence builds the conclusion. The logical sequence at the series level, not section structure. "Start with the reader's experience, ground it in domain-specific evidence, then reveal the cross-domain pattern" is an argument arc. "Section 1 opens with a personal narrative, Section 2 presents healthcare data" is pre-comprehending the article.

- **Thesis elements** — which specific derivations, distinctions, or framework elements this article establishes. These are building blocks for the series, not arguments to be made.

- **Misconception to reframe** — the binary or default frame this article replaces. One sentence.

- **Research to load** — which documents are primary (must-read before drafting) and which are supporting (load as the argument requires). This is a loading manifest, not an interpretation.

- **Cross-domain connections** — how this article connects to other articles in the series. These connections help the drafting agent understand the article's position in the build.

---

## What Series-Level Comprehension Does NOT Produce

- **Per-section argument sequences.** The drafting skill's Comprehend phase builds these. Pre-building them at series design time means the drafting agent inherits compressed argument logic instead of reasoning through the evidence fresh.

- **Section structures.** How the article is organized internally is determined by the story, the emotional arc, and the single thread — all discovered during Comprehend and Design in the drafting skill.

- **Evidence selections for specific sections.** "Use the UCSF study in Section 2" over-specifies. "The UCSF study is primary evidence for this article's thesis" is the right level.

- **Narrative inventories.** Which first-person accounts or specific moments to use, and where — this is the drafting skill's per-section protocol.

---

## The Comprehension Process

### Step 1: Read all research documents through

Before assigning anything to articles, read all available research. Note what the research makes visible as a body — not what individual documents argue, but what patterns emerge across documents.

### Step 2: Identify the series arc

What does the reader understand after the full series that they couldn't understand from any single article? This is the series-level contribution. The individual articles are the steps that build to this understanding.

### Step 3: Find natural article boundaries

Articles divide where the reader needs a new frame — not where the topic changes. A new article begins when the next step in the series build requires the reader to enter through a different experience or question than the one the current article is working through.

### Step 4: For each article, identify the structural payload

What does the reader carry forward from this article? What would they be unable to understand in later articles without it? This is the necessity test at the series level.

### Step 5: Assign research to articles

Primary research: documents the article must engage with to earn its thesis. Supporting research: documents that strengthen the argument but aren't structurally necessary. Background: documents that provide context only if needed.

The assignment is a loading manifest. Do not interpret the research for the article — that's the drafting skill's comprehension phase.

### Step 6: Check the build

Read the series map entries in order. Does each article build on what came before? Could an article be removed without breaking the series build? If yes, it doesn't earn its place — merge its payload into another article or cut it.

---

## Working With the Author

Series-level comprehension is inherently interactive. The author has context the research doesn't contain — what matters to them, what surprised them in the research process, where they see the story going.

**Key interaction points:**

- After Step 2 (series arc): "Here's what I see this research making visible. Is this the series you want to write, or do you see a different arc?"
- After Step 3 (article boundaries): "Here's where I see the natural breaks. Do these match how you see the reader's journey?"
- After Step 5 (research assignment): "Here's what I've flagged as primary for each article. Are there documents I'm underweighting or overweighting?"

The author's direction at these points reshapes the entire series architecture. Getting this right before producing the artifacts saves the most time.
