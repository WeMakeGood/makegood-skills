# Drafting Architecture

Re-read this file before each drafting and editorial phase. It contains the reasoning principles that govern how evidence becomes prose.

---

## Voice Profile and Writing Standards

The voice profile and writing standards module load in every phase — they inform connection-finding in Comprehend and structural decisions in Design. They reload fresh at the point of generation (Draft and Editorial phases), where context-window position matters most. The voice profile determines *whose* voice leads the article. The writing standards module determines what publication-level craft rules govern the writing. They are complementary and non-overlapping.

---

## Writing From a Voice Profile

The voice profile is a role adoption document. It describes a person — not a style. The agent's job is to inhabit the role, then write. The gates are diagnostic tests that confirm the role is active, not checklists of features to reproduce.

**Four failure modes:**
1. **Feature-matching:** The agent scans the profile for stylistic features (short sentences, action verbs, dry humor), then writes prose that performs those features without occupying the role. The result reads like a checklist of style traits executed by an AI.
2. **Intellectual understanding:** The agent reads the profile, understands it, and writes a competent essay that incorporates some of its ideas. The result sounds measured, earnest, and even — a generic voice with occasional profile-like flourishes.
3. **Ungoverned role adoption:** The agent inhabits the role but drops the guardrails — fabricating details, inventing quotes, or extrapolating beyond sources to make the voice sound more natural or authoritative. The result sounds like the person but contains unsourced claims.
4. **Prohibition without process:** The agent loads the guardrails, understands them, and restates them as prohibitions ("do not fabricate"). But prohibitions don't interrupt generation — the agent generates prose in narrative flow and invents connective details (a day of the week, a setting, a feeling) because the prohibition requires remembering a constraint mid-generation rather than completing an upstream step that makes the violation impossible. The fix is not a stronger prohibition. It is an upstream step: identify available narrative material *before* writing, then generate only from that inventory.

Modes 1 and 2 fail because the agent treats the profile as a *description to approximate* rather than a *role to inhabit*. Mode 3 fails because the agent treats role adoption as permission to relax all constraints. Mode 4 fails because the agent converts process gates into named prohibitions — exactly what F0's own architecture warns against. The fix for 3 and 4: the per-section protocol's step 4 (identify available narrative material) creates the upstream sequence that makes fabrication architecturally difficult rather than merely prohibited.

**The generation process:**
1. **Read the role block.** The article plan contains a `<role>` block written during Design — a first-person identity assignment. This is who you are for this article. It was written when the voice profile was fresh and establishes the identity the Draft session inhabits.
2. **Re-read the voice profile for depth.** The role block is the assignment; the voice profile is the depth. Read for the person — their relationship to the subject, where their authority comes from, how they think, what they care about. Do not scan for features to reproduce.
3. **Inventory available material.** Before writing each section, identify the narrative details that exist in the research: first-person accounts, specific experiences, described moments, personal details. List them. This inventory is exhaustive — it is the only narrative material available for generation. This step is what makes F0 Process Gate 1 operational during drafting rather than aspirational.
4. **Write from the role.** Each sentence is generated by the person established in the role block, using only the inventoried material. Not checked against the profile afterward — produced from inside the role.
5. **Test with the gates.** After writing each section, use the profile's gates as diagnostic tests:
   - **Gate 2 (sentence architecture):** Did the role naturally produce these sentence structures? Or did you write default prose and check it against a list?
   - **Gate 3 (domain stance):** Does this sound like someone inside the field? Or an essayist narrating from a distance?
   - **Gate 4 (signature texture):** Are the person's patterns present because the role produced them? Or because you inserted them?
6. **If a gate test fails:** Do not insert the missing feature. The gate failure means the role slipped — return to the role and regenerate from the inventoried material.

**When the voice slips:** The symptom is prose that sounds correct and competent but could have been written by anyone. Do not swap individual words or insert signature features — that produces prose that is technically compliant but generatively dead. Return to the role and rewrite as the person.

**Generative modes:** Most voice profiles define multiple modes (analytical, narrative, direct). The Design phase determines which mode leads each section. Transitions between modes should follow the article's emotional arc, not alternate mechanically.

**Contextual adaptation:** Voice profiles define how the person adapts to different contexts (peer exchange, teaching, professional). The audience document determines which adaptation applies. An article for leaders encountering an argument for the first time is closer to "group decision-making" or "teaching" mode than to "philosophical exchange" — even when the content is philosophical.

## Evidence Reasoning

### Curate, Don't Embed

The research base is deep. The temptation is to include every finding that supports the argument. The result reads like a research paper — too many studies cited with equal weight, no breathing room.

**Before drafting each section:**
1. Name the 2–3 findings from the research that carry the structural argument for that section
2. Those findings get prose treatment — described, contextualized, connected to the reader's experience
3. Everything else from the research for that section is available for hyperlinking only

This selection happens before writing, not during revision.

### Layer, Don't Linearize

Introduce a concept through the reader's entry point → ground it with a specific research finding → return to the concept with the evidence doing the explanatory work.

The pattern: recognition → evidence → understanding. The reader sees something familiar, encounters data that reframes it, then sees the familiar thing differently.

### Follow Dense Evidence With Connection

After a passage presenting research findings, write a passage connecting those findings to the reader's experience. The article alternates between evidence and recognition — the reader sees the data, then sees their own situation in it.

### Source Hyperlinking

Every sourced finding gets an inline hyperlink. The research base contains URLs for every source; use them. Describe what was found and link to the source. Do not use academic author-name citation style — the reader cares about the finding, not the researchers' names.

## Narrative Construction

### The Story Question

"What happened — or is happening — that this article makes visible?" Not "what is the thesis" but "what's the story that earns the thesis?" The reader arrives at a thesis through narrative that makes the thesis feel inevitable.

### The Emotional Arc

Where is the reader at the beginning — emotionally, not informationally? What do they feel, what do they believe? Where are they at the end? What shifted? The emotional arc determines structure and pacing.

### The Single Thread

One narrative thread connects the evidence. Not "the argument" — the thread. The thing you could describe in one sentence to someone at dinner. If the evidence doesn't connect along a single thread, the article reads as a data arrangement, not a story.

### Section Transitions

Each section's closing creates the question the next section answers. The transition is the question, not a bridge phrase. "Now let's turn to..." and "Another important consideration..." are fill.

### Closing Question

Close the article with a question, not a summary. The question applies the article's frame to the reader's own situation. It must genuinely require thought — if the "right" answer is obvious, the question isn't working.

## Article Structure

The principles above describe what narrative elements to build. This section describes how those elements hold together as a whole. An article is not a sequence of sections — it is a single structure where each part exists because the other parts require it.

### The Structural Question

Every article resolves to one question that every section answers from a different angle. Not a thesis — a question. The article doesn't state this question. It makes the reader ask it by the end of the opening, then answers it through the structure.

Before drafting, name the structural question in one sentence. If you can't, the article doesn't have one yet. The structural question is different from the thesis — the thesis is the answer; the structural question is what makes the reader need the answer.

### The Necessity Test

Each section exists because removing it would break the reader's ability to understand a later section. This is structural dependency, not logical sequence. Historical context isn't background — it's what makes the current situation legible. Policy data isn't a separate section — it lives inside narrative because the narrative IS the evidence.

**Process gate:** Before drafting each section, state what the reader could NOT understand without it. If the answer is "nothing — they'd just miss some supporting evidence," the section doesn't earn its place. Cut it or merge its evidence into a section that does earn its place.

### Evidence Serves Structure, Not Coverage

Evidence appears because it advances the structural question, not because it supports the thesis. Research findings enter through the door the narrative has opened, not through their own door. If a finding doesn't serve the section's structural role, it belongs in a hyperlink, not in prose.

### Movement, Not Arrangement

The article moves the reader from one understanding to a different one. It does not arrange information by topic. The movement has direction, describable in one phrase:

- Specific → Structural → Universal
- Conventional wisdom → Evidence of change → New reality
- Personal experience → Systemic evidence → Ironic closure
- Present crisis → Historical depth → Earned judgment

**Process gate:** Before drafting, describe the article's movement in one phrase. If you can't, the sections are arranged by topic rather than building toward transformation. Restructure until the movement is clear.

### The Opening Earns the Closing

The opening establishes a frame that the closing transforms. Not "returns to" — transforms. The reader arrives at the closing with understanding they didn't have at the opening. If the closing could exist without the middle, the article hasn't earned it.

### The Frame Never Disappears

The opening frame — a person, an event, a question — remains present throughout the article. When policy analysis leaves the person's experience, when historical depth stops returning to the present crisis, when research loses its connection to the author's search — the article becomes a different genre. The frame is what makes it a story rather than a report.

## The Reframe, Not the Argument

When the article encounters a binary discourse, do not argue against either position. Reframe so the actual shape becomes visible. Spend more time on what something IS than what it ISN'T — anticipate the misconception, correct it briefly, then devote the majority of space to the affirmative.

## Reader Entry Points

**Before drafting each section, state:** "The reader already knows from their own work that [specific experience]."

This is the experience the section builds on — the thing the reader recognizes before the section names the structure behind it. Draft from that entry point.

**Opening:** The first two paragraphs connect to the reader's search intent. Start with the interesting part.

---

## What the Outline Is and Isn't

The series outline provides per-article argument guidance: thesis, argument arc, misconception to reframe, keyword targets, series build position. It does not prescribe section structure.

The outline is an evidence plan organized by logical completeness. The draft is organized by the story. If the outline's sequence serves the story, follow it. If the most compelling content would be buried after extensive setup, restructure.

Design includes an alternative-structure requirement specifically because the outline's order may not serve the reader's experience.

---

## Compressed Formulas

Research documents contain synthesized conclusions — landing points earned through pages of evidence. Copying them into the article without the evidence produces assertions the reader has no reason to accept.

When you encounter a compressed formula in the research (a conclusion stated as a given), trace it back to the evidence that earned it. State the argument in terms of the specific situation the article has developed, not in the research document's summary language.
